\section{Classical results}
\label{sec:classical-results}

Results below are production ensembles at $N=3000$ quarks (48 events per
expansion-rate point; the multiquark-favorable ``corner'' uses
$\alpha_s=0.6$, $H_0=0.05\,c/\fm$, and twelve injected $c\bar c$ pairs,
24 events), validated against $N=604$ pilots: the per-quark meson-bound
fraction agrees across the factor-of-five volume change ($12.9\%$ vs
$13.1\%$ at $H_0=0.1\,c/\fm$), as do baryon fractions within errors.

\subsection{Static validation and the correlated gas}

\begin{figure}[t]
\includegraphics[width=\columnwidth]{figs/fig2_static.pdf}
\caption{Static diquark-formation probability
[Eq.~\eqref{eq:pstatic}, hard wall, $k=\sqrt2$] versus per-species
density: Lebed's counting ($n_1{=}3n_2$) and the discrete-color counting
($n_1{=}2n_2$), analytic curves with ideal-gas Monte Carlo points; crosses
are Table~2 of Ref.~\cite{Lebed:2016jvq}.  The countings coincide in the
dilute (purely geometric) limit, cross at intermediate density, and
saturate at $51.5\%$ vs $41.4\%$.}
\label{fig:static}
\end{figure}

Our Lebed-mode Monte Carlo reproduces the published hard-wall value at
reference density, $50.45\pm0.12\%$ vs $50.29\%$, and
Fig.~\ref{fig:static} extends the comparison across five decades of
density in both counting modes.

Beyond the ideal gas, we apply the same estimator to the
\emph{interacting} equilibrated configurations at the simulation's own
density ($n_0R^3=1$, $\Gamma\approx0.8$).  The paired
(equilibrated$-$ideal, same configurations) shift is
$+0.028\pm0.035$ (Lebed counting) and $+0.016\pm0.029$ (discrete
counting): interaction correlations move the static probability by at
most a few percentage points at this coupling, with a positive central
value.  \todo{production statistics for a definitive sign; $k$-dependence
panel.}

\subsection{Species formation in the expanding plasma}

\begin{figure}[t]
\includegraphics[width=\columnwidth]{figs/fig5_scan_H0.pdf}
\caption{Species yields per event versus expansion rate $H_0$
($N=3000$, 48 events/point, bootstrap errors).  Faster quenches capture
fewer mesons; the tetraquark yield is flat within errors across the
factor-of-four range of quench rates.}
\label{fig:scanH0}
\end{figure}

\begin{figure}[t]
\includegraphics[width=\columnwidth]{figs/fig4_species_t.pdf}
\caption{One event at default parameters ($N=604$, $H_0=0.1\,c/\fm$):
cooling history and bound-cluster counts per snapshot.  Mesons condense

\label{fig:speciest}

Across $H_0 = 0.05$--$0.20\,c/\fm$ the meson yield falls from
$207.4\pm1.7$ to $181.9\pm1.5$ per event, (anti)baryons sit at
$2.3$--$2.9$ per species, and --- notably --- the tetraquark yield is
\emph{flat} at $0.58\pm0.10$ per event across the full factor of four
in quench rate (Fig.~\ref{fig:scanH0}).  Five-quark states appear at
every expansion rate once both charge states are counted: $2$, $3$, and
$3$ (anti)pentaquarks in the three 48-event ensembles, i.e.\
$\simeq0.04$--$0.06$ per event with no significant $H_0$ trend.  A
single-event history is shown in Fig.~\ref{fig:speciest}.

\subsection{Constituent-number penalty}

\begin{figure}[t]
\includegraphics[width=\columnwidth]{figs/fig6_penalty.pdf}
\caption{Yield per event (per species; particle and antiparticle states
averaged) versus constituent number at two couplings, $N=3000$.  All
four points are now measurements at both couplings.  The ladder is
$69\times$ ($2\to3$), $4.9\times$ ($3\to4$), and $19\times$ ($4\to5$)
at $\Gamma\approx0.8$, and $61\times$, $4.8\times$, $26\times$ at the
charm-enriched $\Gamma\approx1.2$ corner.}
\label{fig:penalty}
\end{figure}

The production ensembles resolve the full ladder
(Fig.~\ref{fig:penalty}): the meson$\to$baryon step costs
$\sim65\times$, but the ladder is \emph{not} geometric beyond it ---
adding a fourth constituent to a baryon-mass cluster costs only
$\sim5\times$, while the fifth costs $\sim20\times$.  The cheap $3\to4$
step reflects the combinatorics of the discrete-color model: a
tetraquark can be assembled from two already-attractive $q\bar q$ or
$qq$ sub-systems, whereas the first baryon requires three mutually
attractive quarks.  This deviation from the naive
constant-penalty-per-constituent expectation of coalescence
\cite{Fries:2008hs,ExHIC:2010gcb} is a concrete, testable output.

The charm-enriched corner delivered manifest flavor exotics: in 24
events, \emph{seven} tetraquarks and one antipentaquark whose net
flavor cannot be carried by any $q\bar q$ or $qqq$ assignment --- the
classical realization of the Tier-1 charge-exotic classification of
Sec.~\ref{sec:tier1} --- for an exotic-tetraquark yield of
$0.29\pm0.11$ per event.  \todo{flavor decomposition of the exotic
clusters ($cc\bar q\bar q$-class vs light-flavor-exotic) from the

\subsection{Annihilation timescales and flavor chemistry}

\begin{figure}[t]
\includegraphics[width=\columnwidth]{figs/fig7_scan_tau.pdf}
\caption{Species yields versus bound-pair lifetime $\tau_{\rm bound}$ at
pure photon branching ($P_\gamma=1$).  Meson depletion is mild because
the bound-pair duty cycle inside $d_{\rm ann}$ is small (effective
lifetimes $\gg$ the simulated window); the apparent (anti)baryon
enhancement at finite $\tau_{\rm bound}$ is a $\sim2\sigma$-per-channel
effect requiring production statistics.}
\label{fig:scantau}
\end{figure}

At $P_\gamma=1$ every annihilation removes a pair permanently: $84\pm3$
photons escape per event, dominated by hot-phase scattering annihilation
rather than bound-state decay (Fig.~\ref{fig:scantau}).  The
lifetime calibration makes this quantitative: prepared bound mesons with
a reference orbit (duty cycle $0.386$ inside $d_{\rm ann}$) decay with
$\tau_{\rm eff} = (7.2\text{--}7.3)\,\tau_{\rm bound}$, measured across a
factor of four in $\tau_{\rm bound}$ and consistent with the predicted
$3/\text{duty} = 7.8$; wider orbits have smaller duty cycles and
correspondingly longer effective lifetimes, which is why late-forming
mesons deplete only mildly within the simulated window.

\begin{figure}[t]
\includegraphics[width=\columnwidth]{figs/fig8_chemistry.pdf}
\caption{Chemistry-active slow-quench event ($H_0=0.02\,c/\fm$,
$\lambda_{\rm scatt}=20\,c/\fm$, $P_\gamma=0.3$,
$\tau_{\rm bound}=10\fm/c$): (a) net strangeness production through
thermal-weight re-injection (the classical strangeness-enhancement
analog), $N_s/N$ rising from $0.232$ to $0.251$; (b) escaped-photon pair
energies, peaked near $2m_q$ plus thermal motion.}
\label{fig:chem}
\end{figure}

With the gluon channel active, flavor chemistry operates:
Fig.~\ref{fig:chem} shows a slow-quench event in which re-injection at
falling $\Teff$ raises the strange fraction by two percentage points
while 55 annihilations escape to the photon ledger.

\subsection{The diquark question, dynamically}

The production ensembles track $\sim750$ baryon formation histories.
Across every point --- expansion rates spanning a factor of four and
couplings up to $\Gamma\approx1.2$ --- the diquark-first fraction is
\begin{equation}
f_{\rm diquark\text{-}first} = 0.91\text{--}0.95 \pm 0.02,
\end{equation}
($0.92$, $0.95$, $0.93$ across the $H_0$ scan; $0.91$ at the corner;
Fig.~\ref{fig:dqfirst}).  Two statements follow.  First, dynamical
evolution \emph{amplifies} the static nearest-neighbor probability of
Ref.~\cite{Lebed:2016jvq} by more than a factor of three (static band
$26$--$31\%$ at this density): once a diquark binds, it survives to
seed a baryon.  Second, the fraction is now measurably \emph{below}
unity: a small but resolved population of baryons assembles all at
once, without a persistent two-quark intermediate.

\begin{figure}[t]
\includegraphics[width=\columnwidth]{figs/fig9_dqfirst.pdf}
\caption{Diquark-first fraction of baryons versus expansion rate at two
couplings, compared with the static nearest-neighbor band evaluated at
the simulation density ($n_0R^3=1$; band spans the Lebed and
discrete-color countings).  Dynamics amplifies the static probability
toward unity.}
\label{fig:dqfirst}
\end{figure}
